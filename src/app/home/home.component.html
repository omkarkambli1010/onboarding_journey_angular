What is Demat Account?


A Demat account, short for "Dematerialized account," is like a digital vault for your stocks and securities. It holds them in electronic form instead of physical certificates. It makes buying, selling, and transferring shares easier in the stock market, eliminating the need for paperwork. It is where your investments are stored and managed electronically.

Dematerialisation is the process by which physical certificates are converted into electronic balances. That way, you do not have to hold physical certificates of shares or bonds, but instead hold them just as a credit entry in your demat account opened with NSDL or with CDSL.

FAQs

Imagine your Demat account as a secure digital locker for your investments, like stocks and bonds. Instead of physical certificates, these investments are stored electronically. When you buy or sell stocks, it is like moving them in or out of this digital locker. Your Demat account keeps track of all these transactions, making it convenient and efficient to manage your investments in the stock market without dealing with paperwork or physical documents.

To hold electronic securities e.g., shares, bonds, mutual funds, IPOs, etc., in electronic format, you require a Demat account.

SEBI regulations do not permit you to trade equities without demat account, be it intraday or delivery. Similarly, index ETFs and other ETFs also mandatorily need a demat account for holding them.

If you want to invest in any IPO, then having a demat account is mandatory. IPO shares are only allotted in demat form. In fact, if you do not have an active demat account then your IPO application itself is not valid.

There are two types of demat accounts – Individual account and Non-Individual account. For individuals, the demat account can be classified based on residence of the account holder.

  • Demat account for Resident Indians.
  • Repatriable & Non-Repatriable Accounts for NRIs

BSDA account for RI – The Basic Service Demat Account (BSDA) is for small investors whose portfolio size does not exceed value of total Rs. 4 lakhs. BSDA account holder can hold up to Rs. 2 lakh in debt securities and up to Rs. 2 lakh in other-than-debt securities. An investor can only have one BSDA demat account across all depositories. It is a free demat account opening; the demat account charges are very nominal on an annualized basis.

Regular Demat account for RI – If BSDA accounts cross the limit, they are automatically classified as regular accounts. Here there is no upper limit on value of holdings.

Repatriable account for NRI – Through this account, the demat gains can be repatriated (up to US$ 1 million in each fiscal year) to the foreign country where the NRI resides. A repatriable demat account has to be mandatorily linked to an NRE (Non-Resident External) account.

Non-repatriable demat account for NRI – This account is for NRIs who do not want to repatriate the gains of demat abroad as this account does not permit free transfer of funds outside India. These non-repatriable demat accounts are linked to NRO (Non-Resident Ordinary) accounts.

Dematerialization is the process by which physical share certificates are converted into electronic entries, where shares are held as digital credits in the demat account.

Depository is the central nodal agency which is also a quasi-regulator. In India, there are 2 principal depositories, viz., NSDL and CDSL.

Depository participants or DP are the brokers and banks authorized by NSDL and CDSL to open demat accounts and administer them on their behalf. They interface with clients.

Lien on demat is when your demat shares are pledged with a bank or financer and the shares cannot be sold without their permission.

Freeze on demat account can be either voluntary or by order of court. If you are travelling abroad for a long time, you can temporarily freeze your demat accounts to block any transactions. This is for your own safety. However, in case of any default or criminal charges against a person, the demat account can be frozen by court order too.

Transmission of demat account is by the normal process of law. When a person dies, the shares in his/her demat account automatically passes on to the legal heir or nominee. That is why nomination for demat account is so important.

Transposition in demat is done when the order of joint holders has to be changed. This is often required when the names on the certificates do not match with the names on the demat account.

Closure of demat account is an application to the DP to close the demat account. It can only be done after all the shares are transferred out of the demat account or they are sold.

A. Yes, shares can be transferred from one Demat account to another. Here is the process of transfer in brief.

Step 1 - The investor fills the Delivery Instruction Slip (DIS) and submits it to the current broker.
Step 2 - The broker forwards the DIS form or request to the depository.
Step 3 - The Depository will transfer your existing shares to the Demat account.
Step 4 - Once the transfer of all the shares is completed, the same will be reflected in the investor’s new Demat account.
